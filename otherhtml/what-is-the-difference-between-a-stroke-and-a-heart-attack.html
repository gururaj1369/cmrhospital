What is the difference between a stroke and a heart attack?

Search By Specialities

What is the difference between a stroke and a heart attack?

Both heart attacks and strokes are significant medical illnesses. The symptoms appear unexpectedly and frequently appear out of nowhere. Their long-term health consequences have the potential to be life-changing. They have the same underlying aetiology and many of the same risk factors, but that's about where the similarities stop. Learn the difference between a heart attack and a stroke, and how to seek the life-saving medical attention you need if you suspect one is occurring.

 

Which is more dangerous, a heart attack or a stroke?

Coronary heart disease, which causes heart attacks, is the leading cause of mortality. Heart attacks account for 13% of all adult fatalities. Strokes, the fifth leading cause of mortality in the World, account for around 5% of all adult fatalities. This indicates that heart attacks are more lethal than strokes, despite the fact that both may result in serious long-term disability for survivors.

It's difficult to tell if heart attacks are more dangerous than strokes – or vice versa. Heart attacks and strokes, like many medical diseases, may vary from moderate and even quiet to life-changing and even fatal. Many strokes may impair your capacity to think, walk, and care for your daily needs, making the long-term repercussions for some stroke survivors very difficult to manage.

What are the causes of heart attacks and strokes?

Most heart attacks and strokes are caused by atherosclerosis, which is the development of fatty plaque inside certain blood channels called arteries. Plaque is a gritty, thick substance composed of cholesterol, lipids, calcium, and blood cells. It gradually accumulates and causes arteries in the body to constrict and harden.

A piece of plaque may break off and move downstream, totally cutting off blood flow to an organ or muscle. A heart attack occurs when an artery delivering blood to the heart muscle (a coronary artery) becomes blocked. A stroke may occur if the blockage occurs in the neck or brain.

A word of caution: While all heart attacks are caused by coronary artery blockages, strokes may be caused by both blood vessel blockages and bleeding. Strokes induced by blood clots in the brain are not frequently caused by atherosclerosis.

What are the most prevalent causes of heart attacks and strokes?

Heart attacks and strokes are sometimes grouped together as "cardiovascular disease" since they have similar risk factors such as:

  • Diabetes

  • Blood pressure is too high.

  • High triglyceride levels

  • Obesity and being overweight

  • Using cigarettes

  • A high-saturated-fat, trans-fat, cholesterol, salt, and sugar diet

  • Excessive alcohol consumption

  • a lack of physical activity

  • These risk factors contribute to atherosclerosis over time.

    • How can I tell whether I'm having a heart attack or a stroke?

    Recognizing the signs of a heart attack or stroke as soon as possible is critical to receiving the life-saving treatment you need. The sooner you get therapy, the higher your chances of recovery.

    Signs of a heart attack

  • Chest discomfort or pressure are common signs of a heart attack. The pain is sometimes in the centre of the chest or on the left side. The discomfort might sometimes spread to the arms or jaw. You may also experience shortness of breath, sweating, or nausea. However, not everyone has typical heart attack symptoms. Women, diabetics, and the elderly are more likely to have "atypical" symptoms such as chest tightness and jaw and throat pain.

  • When you experience any of these symptoms with physical effort and they go away with rest, this might be an early warning sign of a heart attack. If this continues, you should seek medical attention to determine if you are at danger of having a heart attack.

    • Symptoms of a stroke

    Stroke symptoms vary greatly depending on which part of the brain is damaged. The most important thing to remember is that stroke symptoms appear suddenly.

    Among the stroke symptoms are:

  • Unexpected fresh perplexity

  • Sudden new difficulty speaking, finding words, or comprehending typical conversation

  • Sudden numbness or weakness on one side of the face or body

  • Slumping, trouble walking, or loss of balance all occur suddenly.

  • A sudden shift in vision

  • A strong headache that seems to appear out of nowhere

    • What are the long-term consequences of a heart attack or stroke?

    Both heart attacks and strokes may be life-altering occurrences, and each survivor's rehabilitation is unique. Most heart attacks leave a person with much less stamina than before, but therapy may relieve the blockage and restore blood flow to the afflicted region of the heart. Your recovery is determined by how healthy you were before to the heart attack, how much of the heart was injured, what treatment you had, and how hard you strive to restore fitness thereafter.

    Because strokes impair brain function, the long-term consequences vary depending on which area of the brain was damaged and how much of the brain was impacted, as well as the rehabilitative treatment you get. The results might range from full recovery to lifelong incapacity.It's a win-win scenario when it comes to avoiding heart attacks and strokes.

    In other words, everything you can do to address modifiable risk factors for atherosclerosis will help avoid heart attacks and strokes.

    In practise, this translates to:

  • Quitting smoking, which may cut your risk by up to 50%

  • Having your blood pressure measured and treated (if high)

  • Being aware of your cholesterol levels and taking actions to improve them

  • Having your blood sugar evaluated for prediabetes or diabetes and taking efforts to correct it

  • If you've previously had a heart attack or stroke, staying on top of regular health checks and taking stroke and heart attack preventative medicine will dramatically lower your chances of having another one.
